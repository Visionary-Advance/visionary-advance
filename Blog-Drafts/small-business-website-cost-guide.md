# How Much Does a Small Business Website Cost in 2026? (Real Numbers)

The honest answer: anywhere from $0 to $50,000. Which is useless. Let's break it into the four ranges that actually exist, what you get at each one, and which one fits your business.

## TL;DR — the four price ranges

| Approach | Upfront cost | Monthly cost | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| DIY template (Wix, Squarespace) | $0–$300 | $20–$50 | Hobbyists, brand-new businesses |
| Freelancer | $500–$3,000 | $0–$30 | Tight budgets, simple needs |
| Small agency / studio | $1,000–$8,000 | $0–$50 | Most small businesses (sweet spot) |
| Full-service agency | $10,000–$50,000+ | $100–$500 | Funded startups, multi-location |

Most small businesses end up in the third row. Here's what actually drives cost.

## What you're really paying for

A small business website is three separate cost components stapled together:

1. **Design** — how it looks, how easy it is to use, how it represents your brand
2. **Development** — how it's built, how fast it loads, how easy it is to update
3. **Strategy** — does it actually convert visitors into customers?

Cheap options tend to skip strategy entirely. Expensive options bundle in things you may or may not need (think: $3,000 photo shoots, brand workshops, animation packages).

The trap: paying for design without strategy gets you a pretty site that doesn't generate leads. Paying for strategy without development gets you a slow site that ranks badly.

## The DIY route: $0–$300 upfront, $20–$50/month

Tools: Wix, Squarespace, GoDaddy Website Builder, Carrd.

**Pros:**
- Cheapest upfront
- You're in control
- Templates are decent in 2026 (much better than 2018)

**Cons:**
- Monthly fees never go away — at $40/month for 5 years, you've paid $2,400
- Limited SEO control (every Squarespace site looks like every other Squarespace site to Google)
- Performance is mediocre (300+ ms slower than custom builds, on average)
- You build it yourself, which is a real time cost

**When it makes sense:** brand new businesses with no revenue yet, side projects, anyone who needs a 1-page presence today.

## The freelancer route: $500–$3,000 upfront

This is where most small businesses *think* they should land, and often regret it.

**Pros:**
- One-time cost, you own the site
- Personal attention from a single person
- Cheaper than a small agency

**Cons:**
- Quality is wildly variable
- Most freelancers can't handle SEO, design, and development equally well
- If they disappear after launch (common), you're stuck
- "Just a few revisions" often turns into scope-creep arguments

**When it makes sense:** when you've worked with a freelancer before and trust them, or when you have an extremely simple need (a 2-page restaurant site, for instance).

The biggest risk: you don't know what you don't know. A freelancer who isn't great at SEO will deliver a site that looks fine but never ranks. You won't realize until 6 months later when you're not getting any organic traffic.

## The small agency route: $1,000–$8,000 upfront

This is where most small businesses get the best ROI in 2026.

**What you typically get for $1,000–$3,000:**
- 3–5 page custom-designed site
- Mobile responsive
- Basic SEO foundation (titles, meta, schema)
- Contact form connected to email
- 30 days post-launch support

**What you typically get for $3,000–$8,000:**
- 6–12 page site
- Full custom design with brand-specific imagery
- Stronger SEO (content plan, local schema, GBP alignment)
- CRM integration (HubSpot, Jobber, Square)
- CMS for self-updates
- 30–60 days post-launch support

You pay once, you own the site, monthly costs are just hosting (~$10) and your domain (~$15/year). At Visionary Advance, our [small business website design](/small-business-website-design) packages start at $1,000 with this model.

## The full-service agency route: $10,000+

Real talk: most small businesses don't need this.

You're paying for project managers, dedicated designers, dedicated developers, brand strategists, content writers, photographers — and the agency's overhead. The work can be excellent. But for a typical small business, the marginal value over a small agency or studio is hard to justify.

**When it makes sense:** funded startups, businesses with $5M+ revenue who need multi-location complexity, regulated industries (healthcare, finance) where compliance matters.

## The hidden costs nobody talks about

Ask any cost question and you'll get the upfront number. Here's what almost no guide mentions:

### 1. Monthly platform fees compound brutally
Squarespace at $40/month is $480/year, $2,400 over 5 years. A custom build for $2,000 with $10/month hosting is $2,600 over 5 years — and you own it.

### 2. Theme/plugin fees on WordPress and Shopify
A "$300 WordPress site" usually has $200/year in plugin licenses. A "$500 Shopify theme" plus apps adds up to $80–$200/month.

### 3. Updates, maintenance, security
Templates auto-update. Custom sites need maintenance — typically $50–$300/month if you outsource it, $0 if you keep it simple.

### 4. Content (the time sink)
Most quotes don't include writing your content. If you don't have copy ready, expect $500–$2,000 extra for a copywriter, or 20+ hours of your own time.

### 5. Photography
Stock photos are obvious. Real photography of your business runs $500–$2,000 for a half-day shoot. Worth it.

## So what should *you* pay?

A reasonable target for most small businesses:

- **$1,000–$2,500 one-time** for the site itself (5–8 pages, custom design, real SEO foundation)
- **$10–$20/month** for hosting + domain
- **$300–$500/month** for an SEO retainer if growth is a priority
- **Optional**: $50–$100/month for ongoing maintenance

Total Year-1 cost: roughly $2,500–$6,500 depending on how aggressive you go on SEO.

Compare that to spending $40/month on Wix forever and never ranking, or $15,000 with an agency that bundles services you don't need, and the middle ground is clearly the right answer for most.

## What to ask before paying anyone

1. Do I own the site at the end, or am I locked into a platform?
2. What's the *all-in* monthly cost (hosting, plugins, license fees)?
3. What SEO is included — and what's the proof you can deliver it?
4. What happens if you disappear / get hit by a bus?
5. Can I update content myself without paying you each time?

If you can't get clear answers to those five questions, walk away.
