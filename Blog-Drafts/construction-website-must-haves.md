# What Makes a Good Construction Website? 9 Must-Haves in 2026

A good construction website does one job well: it turns a stranger searching at 9 PM on a phone into a qualified lead who's ready to talk price. Most contractor websites fail at this — they look like a brochure, not a sales tool.

Here's what separates the websites that fill your pipeline from the ones that just sit there.

## 1. A clear "what we do, where we work" headline

The first thing a visitor sees should answer two questions in five seconds:

- What does this company actually do?
- Do they work in my area?

A headline like "Custom Home Builder Serving Eugene, Springfield & Lane County" beats "Quality You Can Trust" every time. Specific beats clever.

## 2. Project gallery with location and category

Photos sell construction work. But not just any photos — your gallery needs:

- Location tags (city or neighborhood)
- Service category (kitchen remodel, roofing, deck, etc.)
- Before/after pairings where relevant
- Honest captions describing scope and timeline

A homeowner shopping for a deck wants to see *deck projects in their county*. Not stock photos, not your one ADU you're proud of from 2019.

## 3. Mobile-first design (because that's where the lead is)

Over 60% of contractor website traffic is on mobile. Most leads happen at night, on a phone, after dinner. If your site:

- Takes 5+ seconds to load
- Has tap targets you can't hit with a thumb
- Buries the contact button in a hamburger menu

…you're losing those leads to whichever competitor's site loads cleanly.

## 4. A click-to-call button on every page

On mobile, the highest-converting CTA on a contractor site is the phone number. Not a form, not an email — a tap-and-call button.

Every page should have:

- A sticky header with phone number
- A click-to-call CTA in the hero
- A second click-to-call after every major content block

Trying to get every visitor to fill out a form is leaving money on the table.

## 5. Real testimonials with names, photos, and project context

"Great work!" - Mike S. is worthless. Real testimonials have:

- Full first name and last initial
- The specific project (kitchen remodel in Springfield, deck in Eugene)
- Ideally a photo of the homeowner or the finished project
- A quote that mentions a specific detail (timeline, communication, surprise issue handled)

If you can pull reviews directly from Google or Yelp via API, even better — that's social proof Google itself can verify.

## 6. Service-area pages that target local SEO

If you serve five cities, you need five service-area pages — not one "we serve the whole tri-county area" mention buried in a footer.

Each page should be unique (not duplicate content) and cover:

- The cities/neighborhoods you serve in that area
- Projects you've completed there
- Local landmarks or context that prove you actually work there
- A call to action specific to that area

This is how you rank for "general contractor [city]" searches — which are the highest-intent queries in your market.

## 7. Schema markup (the SEO step nobody does)

Schema markup is structured data that tells Google exactly what your business is, where you work, and what services you offer. Most contractor websites have zero schema, which is why they get out-ranked by competitors with worse content.

The minimum schema set for a construction website:

- `LocalBusiness` (or `GeneralContractor`)
- `Service` for each trade you offer
- `AggregateRating` if you have reviews
- `BreadcrumbList` for navigation
- `FAQPage` for any FAQ section

Implemented right, schema is the difference between showing up in a Google "Local Pack" result vs. showing up on page 4.

## 8. A way to capture leads that don't call

Some leads aren't ready to call. They want to send a quick message and hear back. Your form needs:

- Name, email, phone (phone optional)
- Project type dropdown (matches your services)
- Timeline ("ASAP", "1-3 months", "just researching")
- Message field
- Direct connection to your CRM (Jobber, HubSpot, or at minimum email to multiple inboxes)

The form should be on every page — minimum at the bottom of long pages, ideally also in a sticky sidebar on desktop. Don't make people hunt for "Contact" in the navigation.

## 9. Real Core Web Vitals (not just "looks fast")

Google has confirmed: site speed is a ranking factor. Specifically, Core Web Vitals — Largest Contentful Paint, Interaction to Next Paint, Cumulative Layout Shift.

A good construction website hits:

- LCP under 2.5 seconds
- INP under 200ms
- CLS under 0.1
