# Law Firm Website Design Best Practices: 12 Rules That Actually Drive Cases

A law firm website has one job: convert a stranger in legal trouble into an intake call. Most attorney websites fail at this — not because they look bad, but because they ignore the specific rules that make a *legal* site convert.

Here are the 12 rules we follow on every law firm site we build.

## 1. Lead with the practice area, not the firm name

A homeowner facing foreclosure doesn't care that you're "Smith, Smith & Associates, Established 1972." They care: do you handle foreclosure defense?

Your hero headline should make the practice area unmistakable in the first 3 seconds:

> "Personal Injury Attorneys Serving the Bay Area"
> "DUI Defense in San Antonio — 24/7 Consultations"

Specific beats prestigious. Especially in legal.

## 2. Click-to-call above the fold on every page

The single highest-converting CTA on a law firm site is the phone number — not the form. Most prospective clients searching for a lawyer want to talk to a human now, not fill out an intake form.

Every page needs:

- Phone number in the sticky header
- Click-to-call CTA in the hero
- Phone number in the footer

For mobile, every phone number must be `tel:` linked. We see firms lose 30%+ of mobile leads to broken or non-tappable phone numbers.

## 3. Real attorney bios with real photos

Stock photo lawyers in suits standing in front of bookshelves are a credibility killer. Prospects can spot stock photography in milliseconds.

Each attorney bio needs:

- Real, professional headshot (not stock)
- Education, bar admissions, years practicing
- Specific case types they handle
- One personal detail that makes them human (volunteer work, specialization origin story)
- Direct contact info

Bonus: schema markup using the `Person` and `hasOccupation` types so Google can build a Knowledge Panel for each attorney.

## 4. Practice-area pages — one per service, indexed individually

If you handle 6 practice areas, you need 6 distinct pages — not one "Our Services" page with a bulleted list.

Each practice area page should have:

- 800–1,500 words of unique content
- Specific case-result examples (anonymized if needed)
- An intake CTA at top, middle, and bottom
- Practice-area-specific schema (`LegalService`)
- FAQ schema for common questions about that practice area

This is how you rank for "personal injury attorney [city]" queries — the highest-intent searches in your market.

## 5. ADA / WCAG 2.1 AA compliance is non-negotiable

ADA lawsuits against law firms are common. The irony: many firms write demand letters for ADA violations on their *clients'* websites while their own site fails the same tests.

Minimum compliance requirements:

- Color contrast ratios of 4.5:1 for body text
- All images have descriptive alt text
- Forms have proper `<label>` associations
- Site is fully keyboard-navigable (no mouse required)
- All interactive elements have visible focus states
- No reliance on color alone to convey information
- Semantic HTML (`<h1>`, `<h2>`, `<nav>`, `<main>`)

This is also a Google ranking factor — accessible sites tend to score better on Core Web Vitals. We bake this into every law firm site at our [law firm web design service](/law-firm-web-design).

## 6. Bar-rule-compliant testimonials and case results

State bar rules vary, but the general principles:

- Don't make outcome guarantees ("we always win")
- Always include a disclaimer that past results don't predict future outcomes
- Be careful with specific dollar settlements (some states require disclaimers about fees and costs)
- Get written permission before naming a client

Have your firm's compliance lead review the language. Don't trust a generic agency to handle this — they will get it wrong.

## 7. Intake form connected to your CRM

Forms that just send an email get lost. Intake leads need to land in your case management system immediately.

Standard integrations:

- Clio Grow
- MyCase
- HubSpot
- Lawmatics
- Filevine

If you don't have a CRM yet, even a Zapier-routed Google Sheet is better than an email that gets buried by 6 PM.

## 8. Fast load times — sub-2-second LCP

Site speed is a direct ranking factor and an even bigger conversion factor. Every additional second of load time drops conversion by ~7%.

Targets:

- LCP (Largest Contentful Paint): under 2.5 seconds
- INP (Interaction to Next Paint): under 200ms
- CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift): under 0.1

How most firms blow this:

- Massive unoptimized hero photos (5MB+)
- Bloated WordPress themes with 30+ plugins
- Live chat widgets that load 500KB of JavaScript
- Heavy slideshow animations on the homepage

Fix it: optimize images (AVIF/WebP), minimize plugins, lazy-load below-the-fold content.

## 9. Local SEO foundation

Most law firm searches are local: "personal injury lawyer near me", "DUI attorney Houston", "estate planning attorney Boston."

Local SEO basics:

- Google Business Profile claimed and fully filled out (every field)
- LocalBusiness/LegalService schema on every page
- Consistent NAP (Name, Address, Phone) across the entire web
- Citations on legal directories (Avvo, Justia, FindLaw, Martindale)
- Reviews on Google (asking for them is fine, paying for them is not)

A site without local SEO foundation is invisible for ~80% of legal queries.

## 10. Trust signals above the fold

Within the first scroll, a prospect should see at least three trust signals:

- Years in practice ("Trial-Tested Since 2009")
- Awards or recognitions (Super Lawyers, AV-rated, Best Lawyers)
- Bar memberships
- Real client review snippets (with full names where possible)
- Number of cases handled or recovery amount (if bar rules allow)

Don't pile on every signal you have — pick three that matter most for your practice area.

## 11. Mobile-first design (most legal traffic is mobile)

Over 65% of law firm website traffic is on mobile. The site must:

- Load fast on cellular (sub-2s on 4G)
- Have tap targets at least 44px tall
- Show the phone CTA prominently without scrolling
- Avoid hover-only navigation menus (no hovers on mobile)
- Work without pinch-zoom

Test on a real iPhone or Android. Browser dev tools lie about real-device performance.

## 12. Compliant cookie consent and privacy policy

If you serve clients in California, you need a CCPA notice. If you handle EU clients, GDPR. If you collect any contact form data, a privacy policy.

Free tools handle most of this — Cookiebot, OneTrust, or a static privacy policy generator works for most firms. Just don't skip it.
